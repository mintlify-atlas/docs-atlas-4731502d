<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Indo-Pacific Humpback Dolphin, widely known as the **Pink Dolphin**, has a distinctive rounded head, a long slender beak, and 28–38 pairs of teeth on both upper and lower jaws. What makes this species particularly striking is the way its color changes over its lifetime:

    - **Juveniles** — dark gray or black
    - **Sub-adults** — the color fades progressively
    - **Adults** — some individuals become mottled gray-white; in older animals the skin turns a vivid white or bright pink

    This age-related color change is the origin of the popular name "pink dolphin." The species inhabits shallow coastal and estuarine waters, and populations in Thailand are known from both the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea, with the Khanom-Don Sak Bay being a well-documented habitat.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    The Pink Dolphin faces a broad mix of human-driven and natural threats:

    **Irresponsible tourism**
    Tourist boats in areas such as Khanom-Don Sak Bay have been documented feeding dolphins, which alters their natural behavior and exposes them to contaminated food. This is both harmful and prohibited.

    **Fishing gear**
    Accidental entanglement (bycatch) in nets and illegal fishing gear causes injuries and death. Boat propellers also pose a risk to dolphins foraging in areas with heavy vessel traffic.

    **Marine pollution**
    Marine debris, including floating plastic, can be ingested and block the digestive system. Toxins accumulate in the dolphin's body through the food chain over time.

    **Natural predators and illness**
    Illness, age-related weakness, and fleeing from natural predators such as sharks or orcas can drive dolphins into shallow water, resulting in strandings.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    All dolphin and whale species in Thai waters are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535. Hunting, trading, possessing, or breeding any cetacean is strictly prohibited.

    **Stranding response**
    If you find a stranded dolphin, follow this protocol:
    1. Support the animal in the water so it can maintain an upright position. Keep water out of the blowhole at all times.
    2. If the animal is on dry land or a beach, dig support holes under the pectoral fins to relieve pressure on internal organs. Cover the body with wet cloth (leaving the blowhole exposed) and pour water over the animal regularly to keep it cool and moist.
    3. Use a canvas stretcher with carrying poles to move the animal — the equipment must be appropriate for the animal's size to prevent injury during transport.
    4. Take the animal to a rehabilitation pool large enough to allow swimming. A veterinarian should diagnose and treat the animal until it is ready for release.

    **Addressing root causes**
    Long-term population health depends on eliminating upstream threats:
    - Campaign to **stop feeding dolphins in the wild**, especially in the Khanom area.
    - Reduce marine litter — plastic ingestion is a leading cause of death for marine animals.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
