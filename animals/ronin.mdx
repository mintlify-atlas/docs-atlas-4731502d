<Warning>
  The Bowmouth Guitarfish is classified as **Critically Endangered (CR)** globally by the IUCN and is a **protected wildlife species** in Thailand under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (declared in 2018). It is also listed on **CITES Appendix II**. Encounters with wild individuals are increasingly rare.
</Warning>

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Bowmouth Guitarfish belongs to the cartilaginous fish group and has a uniquely striking appearance. Its head is large and wide with a **semicircular, bluntly rounded snout** — the curved bow-shape that gives the species its English name and distinguishes it from the related Shovelnose Ray (which has a pointed snout). Rows of **hard thorny spines** line the eye ridges and run down the center of the back — these "head spines" or "ronin spines" are highly sought after in the wildlife trade.

    Adults grow up to **2.7–3 meters** in length. The species is a benthic (bottom-dwelling) predator that hunts crustaceans — shrimps, mantis shrimps, and crabs — on sandy or muddy seafloors in shallow coral reef areas. It is found at depths from 3 to 90 meters and is distributed across the Indian Ocean and Pacific Ocean. In Thailand it occurs in both the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea, but sightings are infrequent.

    **Reproduction**
    The Bowmouth Guitarfish is viviparous (live-bearing with yolk sac nutrition), producing 2–11 pups per litter. Newborns measure approximately 46–48 cm. Average lifespan is around 35 years.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    **Wildlife trade**
    The most severe and species-specific threat is demand for the **head spines** (the bony dorsal thorns). These are carved into ring heads, bracelets, and protective amulets, based on a belief that they ward off water-related accidents and bring good fortune. The high value of these spines drives targeted fishing. Belly skin is also used for leather goods.

    **Bycatch**
    The Bowmouth Guitarfish's bottom-feeding habits make it highly vulnerable to accidental capture in demersal fishing gear: bottom trawls, purse seines, and crab traps. Entanglement often causes deep lacerations or death. Surviving bycatch animals may have their meat and fins taken.

    **Aquarium capture**
    Live individuals are captured for display in public aquariums because of their unusual appearance, further reducing wild populations.

    **Habitat degradation**
    Coastal development directly impacts the sandy and reef-adjacent habitats the species depends on for feeding.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    Thailand declared the Bowmouth Guitarfish a protected wildlife species under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (Ministerial Regulation 2018). Hunting, trading, or possessing the animal or its products without authorization is prohibited. It is also listed on **CITES Appendix II** to regulate international trade.

    **Rescue operations**
    A reporting network has been established so that responders can be dispatched when a Bowmouth Guitarfish is reported entangled in fishing gear. An example rescue in Phang Nga Province involved an individual whose tail was nearly severed by a crab trap net — officials transported it to a research center for wound debridement and treatment before a supervised recovery.

    **Fisheries management**
    Restrictions on certain fishing gear types — particularly bottom trawls and push nets in coastal zones — reduce the likelihood of accidentally capturing this benthic species, although current restrictions do not yet cover all habitat areas.

    **National action plan**
    The National Plan of Action for the Conservation and Management of Sharks in Thailand (NPOA-Sharks) provides a framework for long-term management of the species.
  </Tab>
