<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Sperm Whale is the **world's largest toothed predator**. Its most distinctive feature is its enormous, box-shaped head — nearly 40% of total body length — which is blunt, wide, and rectangular in profile. The body is slightly laterally compressed, and the skin is dark gray to brownish-gray with a lighter underside. The lower jaw is comparatively narrow and small, and teeth are found **only on the lower jaw**.

    **Behavior**
    Sperm Whales are highly social animals with strong inter-pod bonds. They use **echolocation** to navigate and hunt, emitting burst-pulse sounds — low-frequency clicks of great intensity — to locate and stun prey.

    **Social vulnerability**
    The species' tight social structure is both a strength and a vulnerability. When one member of a pod is in distress, others tend to stay alongside it — a behavior that makes Sperm Whales particularly prone to **mass strandings**.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    **Mass stranding (social behavior)**
    Because Sperm Whales are highly social, mass strandings are a significant risk. A pod may follow a leader that has navigated incorrectly into shallow water, chase prey schools into a coastal embayment, or cluster around an ill or injured pod member — and strand together.

    **Marine pollution and plastic ingestion**
    Cases of Dwarf Sperm Whales (*Kogia sima*, a related species) found stranded and dead in Thailand have been traced to plastic bags compacted in the stomach, causing a fatal blockage. Marine pollution and debris are considered a serious threat to the Sperm Whale group.

    **Other human-driven threats**
    - Noise pollution from petroleum surveys, vessel sonar, and shipping traffic can disrupt echolocation and communication.
    - Accidental entanglement in commercial fishing gear (bycatch).
    - Vessel strikes.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    Sperm Whales are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 (and amendments), with absolute prohibitions on hunting, trading, or possession.

    **Mass stranding response**
    When a group of Sperm Whales strands, the response must be fast and well-coordinated:

    <Steps>
      <Step title="Triage and first aid">
        Prioritize living animals. Support each whale in the water if possible — this reduces stress and relieves pressure on internal organs. Never allow water to enter the blowhole and never cover the blowhole with cloth. Protect eyes from sand and sunlight. Douse the skin with water continuously to reduce body temperature and maintain moisture.
      </Step>
      <Step title="Assess and act">
        If an animal is assessed as strong — able to swim, maintain balance, and breathe independently — begin immediate guided release back to deep water.
      </Step>
      <Step title="Keep families together">
        When releasing a pod, return all animals simultaneously where possible. Mother–calf pairs must be released together; a separated calf will draw the mother back to shore.
      </Step>
      <Step title="Document the event">
        Record all observations, collect biological samples, and perform necropsies on deceased animals to identify cause of death and improve future responses.
      </Step>
    </Steps>
  </Tab>
