Low-Poly Ocean showcases **10 endangered marine species** found in Thai waters, each rendered as a low-polygon art piece composed of dozens to hundreds of triangular shards. The project is built with React and Vite, and uses an interactive detail page at `/detail?id=<animalId>` where you can explore each species' polygon artwork alongside conservation information.

The species range from the massive blue whale — made up of 433 polygon shards — to the relatively compact pink dolphin rendered in 94 shards. Every animal has its own unique color palette and geometry that reflects its real-world appearance.

## Conservation context

Thailand's marine ecosystems — spanning the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea — are home to remarkable biodiversity, but human activity and environmental degradation have placed many species under serious threat.

The key threats faced by these species include:

- **Bycatch** — Accidental entanglement in fishing nets, trawls, and longlines is the leading cause of injury and death for most of the featured species.
- **Illegal fishing** — Blast fishing, poison fishing, and the use of prohibited gear in protected zones devastate entire local populations.
- **Marine debris and pollution** — Floating plastic bags are mistaken for jellyfish and ingested by sea turtles and dugongs. Chemical runoff and wastewater accumulate toxins through the food chain.
- **Coastal development** — Construction along shorelines destroys nesting beaches and feeding grounds, and physical barriers such as erosion-control bamboo walls trap animals during low tides.
- **Noise pollution** — Sonar and seismic surveys for petroleum exploration disrupt the echolocation and navigation of cetaceans, sometimes triggering mass strandings.
- **Vessel strikes** — Propeller injuries are a documented cause of death for sea turtles, dugongs, and cetaceans that surface near boat traffic.
- **Wildlife trade** — High-value body parts — sawfish rostra, manta ray gill rakers, ronin head spines, sea turtle eggs — drive targeted poaching.

All 10 species are protected under Thai law (the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 and its predecessors), and several are listed on CITES Appendix I, which prohibits international commercial trade. Despite these protections, enforcement gaps and low reproduction rates make recovery slow.
