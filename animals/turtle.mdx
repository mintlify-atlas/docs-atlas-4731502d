<Warning>
  The Leatherback Turtle is listed as **reserved wildlife** under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 — the highest tier of domestic protection — and appears on **CITES Appendix I**. Its nesting sites in Thailand are limited to beaches in Phang Nga and Phuket provinces.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Leatherback Turtle is the **largest sea turtle in the world**. Fully grown adults can reach a shell length of 210 cm and weigh up to 900 kg; nesting females typically measure at least 150 cm.

    **Physical characteristics**
    The Leatherback is immediately distinguishable from other sea turtles because its shell carries **no hard scutes**. Instead, the back is covered by a thick, flexible black skin — sometimes dotted with white — with seven pronounced ridges running lengthwise from head to tail. The head is also scaleless, and the upper jaw has three distinct notches. Hatchlings are black with white lines along their ridges; as they grow, white dots spread across the body.

    **Behavior and diet**
    Leatherbacks spend their entire lives in the open sea, returning to shore only to nest. They forage near the surface and feed almost exclusively on **jellyfish and sponges**.

    **Reproduction**
    Females reach sexual maturity at around 13–14 years and nest every 1–3 years, typically between December and February. They prefer to lay eggs in the mid-zone of sandy beaches above the highest-tide line. In Thailand, nesting sites are found only on mainland beaches in Phang Nga and Phuket.

    **Conservation status**
    The Leatherback Turtle is classified as a rare marine species and is protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 (and its amendments).

  <Tab title="Threats">
    Leatherback Turtles and all sea turtles in Thai waters face threats from two main categories:

    **Human-driven threats**

    *Fishing gear (bycatch)*
    Sea turtles frequently become accidentally entangled in fishing gear — gill nets, trawl nets, and longline hooks. Illegal fishing methods such as blast fishing and net use in restricted zones also cause significant casualties.

    *Plastic and pollution*
    Because Leatherbacks eat jellyfish as their primary food, they readily mistake floating plastic bags for prey, ingesting them and suffering fatal digestive blockages. Chemical toxins and wastewater accumulate through the food chain.

    *Coastal development*
    Construction along the Phang Nga and Phuket coastlines and the installation of erosion-control structures can block nesting beaches, preventing hatchlings from reaching the sea and trapping adult females after nesting during falling tides.

    *Vessel strikes*
    Propeller collisions cause severe injury or death to turtles that surface near boat traffic.

    **Natural threats**
    Storms, strong waves, and rapid water-level changes can strand turtles or impact nesting beaches. Natural predators such as sharks and orcas may chase turtles into shallow water, causing strandings or bite injuries. Disease and natural illness are also recorded causes of stranding and death.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    The Leatherback Turtle holds **reserved wildlife status** under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562, carrying severe penalties for hunting, trading, possessing, or breeding the animal — including eggs and carcasses. The species is also on CITES Appendix I, prohibiting international trade.

    **Rescue — fishing gear entanglement**
    - If the turtle is caught in a net, cut away the net fragments and check for wounds. Release the animal immediately if it is healthy.
    - If a hook is visible, do **not** pull the line forcefully — this tears the esophagus. Push the barbed end through until it exits, cut off the barb with pliers, then withdraw the hook backward.
    - If the turtle has swallowed a hook deep into the throat or stomach, do **not** attempt to remove it. Contact officials or a veterinarian immediately for surgical intervention.

    **First aid for stranded turtles**
    1. Move the turtle to a quiet, shaded area.
    2. Cover the shell with a wet cloth to maintain moisture — keep the nostrils clear at all times.
    3. If the turtle appears ill, lethargic, or has severe wounds, use an appropriate carrier or stretcher to transport it. Do not immerse it in water; keep it covered with wet cloth.
    4. If aspiration of water is suspected, tilt the animal so the rear end is higher than the head to allow fluid to drain from the lungs.
    5. Transport time should not exceed 4 hours.

    **Rehabilitation**
    Recovered turtles are nursed in smooth-surfaced tanks that prevent abrasion and are large enough to allow the animal to turn around. Water depth is adjusted so the turtle can surface to breathe without risk of drowning. Food and medication are administered under veterinary supervision until the animal is fit for release.
