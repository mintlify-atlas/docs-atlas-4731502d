<Warning>
  The Giant Manta Ray is classified as **Endangered (EN)** on the IUCN Red List and is listed on CITES Appendix II. In Thailand it is a protected wildlife species under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 (announced in 2018), making it illegal to import, export, or possess the animal or its products without authorization.
</Warning>

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Giant Manta Ray is the **largest ray species in the world**, with a disc width (wingspan) of up to 7–9 meters. It is distinguished from other rays by its **terminal mouth** — positioned at the very front tip of the body rather than underneath — and by the T-shaped black marking on the top of its head.

    The underside of the disc is lighter in color with dark gray-black edges and scattered dark spots. These spots form a unique pattern that allows individual animals to be identified from photographs (Photo ID), a technique used by researchers worldwide.

    Near the base of the dorsal fin sits a small bump — a vestigial, reduced spine — that distinguishes the Giant Manta Ray from related species.

    **Behavior and habitat**
    The Giant Manta Ray is a filter feeder, using its gill rakers to strain plankton, small invertebrates, and fish eggs from the water. It swims near the surface and in deeper water, and undertakes long-distance migrations across ocean basins. It regularly visits **cleaning stations** — underwater pinnacles where small cleaner fish remove parasites from its body.

    **Reproduction**
    Despite a lifespan of around 45 years, the Giant Manta Ray reproduces very slowly: females give birth to a single pup at a time and may not breed again for 4–5 years.

    **Thailand locations**
    In Thai waters, Giant Manta Rays are rarely seen in the Gulf of Thailand but are regularly encountered in the Andaman Sea, particularly around offshore islands and pinnacles: Koh Bon, Koh Tachai, and Richelieu Rock in Phang Nga; Koh Racha Noi in Phuket; and Hin Daeng–Hin Muang in Krabi.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    **Fishing and bycatch**
    The Giant Manta Ray is frequently caught accidentally in commercial fishing gear, including purse seines and trawl nets. Ghost gear — abandoned nets and ropes drifting at sea — wraps around the animal's body and can sever cephalic lobes (the "horns" used to funnel food into the mouth) or fins, leading to infection and death.

    **International trade demand**
    Dried gill rakers are used in traditional Chinese medicine, creating persistent demand that drives targeted fishing. The meat is also consumed in some markets. This direct exploitation is one of the leading causes of population decline.

    **Irresponsible diving tourism**
    Divers chasing or cornering the ray, touching it, or using camera flash disturb feeding and cleaning behavior. These interactions cause stress and may deter the animal from visiting cleaning stations it depends on for parasite removal.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    The Giant Manta Ray is protected under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562. Importation, exportation, and possession of the animal or its products are prohibited without a permit.

    **Research and citizen science**
    The Department of Marine and Coastal Resources (DMCR) works with networks of divers and citizen scientists to build a sighting database and conduct Photo ID analysis using the unique spot patterns on each animal's underside. This data is shared with global organizations such as the Marine Megafauna Foundation (MMF) to track transboundary migration.

    **Rescue operations**
    When a manta ray is reported entangled in fishing gear, a team of trained divers is dispatched to cut away the netting or rope from the animal's body while it remains in the water.

    **Responsible dive tourism guidelines**
    Conservation campaigns promote best-practice guidelines for diving with manta rays:
    - Maintain a respectful distance and never pursue the animal.
    - Do not touch the ray or block its path.
    - Avoid camera flash, which can startle and disrupt natural behavior.
  </Tab>
