<Warning>
  The Dugong is classified as **reserved wildlife** under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562, the highest level of legal protection, and is listed on **CITES Appendix I** (international commercial trade absolutely prohibited). It is considered critically endangered in Thai waters.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Dugong is a **fully aquatic marine mammal** believed to share a common ancestor with elephants approximately 55 million years ago, before its lineage returned to the sea permanently. Its torpedo-shaped body resembles a dolphin's but is rounder and heavier. It has no dorsal fin, but possesses large flukes and a broad tail stock. The skin is thick, ranging from pinkish-gray to brownish-gray, with the underside lighter in color and sparse short hairs scattered across the body — with a dense patch of bristle-like hairs around the mouth.

    **Size**
    Adult dugongs reach 2.5–3 meters in length and weigh 250–420 kg. Newborn calves are 1–1.5 meters and weigh 20–35 kg.

    **Behavior**
    Dugongs are slow swimmers, moving at roughly 1.8–2.2 km/h. Their dense, heavy bones help them stay submerged as they graze along the seafloor. They surface to breathe every 1–2 minutes through nostrils equipped with flap-like valves. Their primary food is seagrass, and they consume 2–5% of their body weight in seagrass daily. Dugongs can live up to 70 years; age can be estimated by counting growth layers in their tusks.

    **Thailand habitat**
    Dugongs are found on both the Gulf of Thailand side (Chonburi, Rayong, Chanthaburi, Trat, Chumphon, Surat Thani, Pattani) and the Andaman side. The highest-density population is in the Koh Libong–Koh Muk area, Trang Province, which historically accounts for 49% of all dugong strandings recorded in Thailand.

  <Tab title="Threats">
    **Human-driven threats**

    *Fishing gear (bycatch)*
    Dugongs feed in the same shallow coastal areas as fishing boats, putting them at high risk of accidental entanglement in nets. Illegal fishing methods — blast fishing, poison fishing, and electrofishing — are especially destructive and account for many strandings each year.

    *Coastal development*
    Structures built over or along waterways, and bamboo wave-break fences installed near feeding grounds, can block the dugong's retreat path during low tides, causing it to become stranded. Coastal construction also degrades the seagrass beds the dugong relies on for food.

    *Marine pollution*
    Dugongs ingest plastic debris and accumulate chemical toxins through the food chain, causing illness and death. Noise pollution from petroleum exploration and fishing vessel sonar disrupts communication and navigation, causing the animals to lose their way.

    *Poaching*
    Post-mortem examinations have documented cases where tusk tips were cut from dead dugongs before reporting, indicating targeted poaching for body parts.

    *Vessel strikes*
    Propeller injuries are a documented cause of death.

    **Natural threats**
    Natural predators (sharks and orcas) can chase dugongs into shallow water, causing decompression sickness or stranding. Storms, rough seas, and rapid tidal changes can also trap feeding animals.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    The Dugong was elevated to **reserved wildlife status** under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562, carrying strict penalties for hunting, trading, or possession. It is also listed on **CITES Appendix I**, prohibiting all international commercial trade.

    **Stranding and rescue protocol**
    The DMCR follows an established rescue manual for stranded rare marine animals:
    1. Activations go through a community reporting network of fishers, residents, and tour operators to get responders on-site quickly.
    2. If the dugong is alive and in good condition, it is immediately released back to sea. If injured or sick (e.g., caught in fishing gear), field first aid is applied and the animal is transported to a rehabilitation pool.
    3. If the animal is dead, a necropsy is performed to determine the exact cause of death — whether deliberate harm, ingested debris, or natural disease — and the data informs future prevention planning.

    **Monitoring and research**
    The DMCR conducts continuous population surveys, habitat mapping, and threat analysis. An online sighting-report system (mes.dmcr.go.th) allows the public to submit observations and helps build the national database.

    **Community engagement**
    Conservation outcomes depend on cooperation with coastal communities, fishing groups, and the tourism industry. Campaigns to reduce marine litter and promote responsible wildlife tourism are ongoing.
