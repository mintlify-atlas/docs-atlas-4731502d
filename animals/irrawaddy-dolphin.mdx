<Note>
  The Irrawaddy Dolphin is listed on **CITES Appendix I** (internationally prohibited from commercial trade) and is classified as a **protected and reserved wildlife species** under Thailand's Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562. It is considered critically endangered in many of its range countries.
</Note>

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Irrawaddy Dolphin is a coastal dolphin with a distinctive rounded, beak-less head, making it immediately recognizable among Thai dolphin species. Its back is blue-gray and its underside is lighter in color. It has a small, rounded dorsal fin positioned mid-body toward the tail, and carries 8–19 upper teeth and 11–18 lower teeth.

    Unlike many open-ocean cetaceans, the Irrawaddy Dolphin is a **resident near-shore species**, meaning it stays close to coastlines and does not undertake long-distance migrations. It favors estuaries, mangrove-lined bays, and shallow coastal waters where it forages for fish and crustaceans.

    In Thailand, populations are found in both the Gulf of Thailand and the Andaman Sea. One of the most documented stranding events involved a group of five Irrawaddy Dolphins stranded along a bamboo wave-break fence in Samut Sakhon Province — a direct consequence of coastal construction near their feeding grounds.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    The Irrawaddy Dolphin faces threats from several directions:

    **Coastal structures**
    Erosion-control structures and bamboo wave-break fences built in feeding areas can act as traps. During low tides, dolphins may fail to retreat to deeper water in time and become stranded.

    **Fishing gear (bycatch)**
    The dolphin's near-shore habitat overlaps heavily with fishing activity. Accidental entanglement in nets — including illegal gear — causes injury, disability, and death.

    **Marine pollution**
    Marine debris and wastewater affect the dolphin both directly (ingestion of plastics) and indirectly (accumulation of toxins through the food chain). Noise pollution from vessel traffic and sonar also disrupts navigation and communication, which can lead to disorientation and stranding.

    **Natural factors**
    Pod dynamics can work against the dolphins: a lead animal that navigates into shallow water may cause the entire group to follow, resulting in a mass stranding.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    Conservation measures for the Irrawaddy Dolphin in Thailand operate at multiple levels:

    **Legal protection**
    The species is strictly protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act, which prohibits hunting, trading, and possession. It is also listed on CITES Appendix I, barring international commercial trade.

    **Stranding response**
    When a stranded dolphin is found, trained responders follow a standardized protocol:
    1. Support the animal in the water so it can breathe normally. Keep the blowhole (on top of the head) clear — never cover it with cloth.
    2. If the animal is on land, dig a pit under each pectoral fin to reduce pressure on internal organs. Drape a wet cloth over the body (avoiding the blowhole) and douse it with water regularly to prevent overheating.
    3. Use appropriate transport equipment — a stretcher or sliding board — to move the animal to a rehabilitation tank.
    4. Keep injured or sick animals under veterinary supervision until they are strong enough to be released.

    **Rehabilitation and release**
    Animals that cannot be immediately released are held in recovery pools under close veterinary care and assessed for readiness before being returned to the sea.
