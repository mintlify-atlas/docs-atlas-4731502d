<Warning>
  The Giant Sawfish is considered **regionally extinct (RE) in Thailand** — no living individual has been reported in the wild for over 30 years. Globally it is classified as **Critically Endangered (CR)** by the IUCN. It is a protected wildlife species in Thailand under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562 and is listed on **CITES Appendix I** (absolute prohibition on international commercial trade).
</Warning>

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Giant Sawfish is the **largest species of sawfish in the world**, reaching up to **7 meters** in length at maturity. Its most iconic feature is the elongated, flattened rostrum — a saw-like snout lined with **16–20 pairs of sharp, evenly spaced teeth**. The upper body is grayish-yellow and the belly is white-cream.

    **Habitat**
    The Giant Sawfish inhabits tropical coastal waters and is found near the seafloor in shallow coral reefs up to about 10 meters deep, in estuaries, and in freshwater rivers. Juvenile fish spend their first 4–5 years in freshwater before migrating to marine environments as they mature.

    **Reproduction**
    The species is slow to mature and slow to reproduce — males are sexually mature at 2.4–3.0 meters, females at 2.8–3.0 meters. Gestation lasts approximately 5 months, and females give birth to 1–13 live pups. Average lifespan is about 35 years.

    **Status in Thailand**
    No live Giant Sawfish have been reliably reported in Thai waters for over 30 years. Today, the species is encountered only as saw rostra preserved in shrines or sold in antique shops.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    **Fishing gear (bycatch)**
    The Giant Sawfish's saw-like rostrum makes it exceptionally prone to entanglement in fishing nets — gill nets, drift nets, trawls, and deep-water traps. Even accidental entanglement frequently results in death or capture, and the animal is then kept rather than released.

    **Targeted poaching and trade**
    Multiple body parts command high prices:
    - The **rostrum (saw)** is highly valued as a wall decoration, collector's item, and protective talisman.
    - **Fins** are used in shark-fin soup.
    - **Meat** is consumed locally.
    - Live individuals are captured for public aquarium display due to their striking appearance.

    **Habitat loss**
    Coastal development and pollution have destroyed the estuarine and shallow coastal habitats that sawfish use for feeding and as juvenile nursery areas. This habitat loss is considered a primary driver of the regional extinction in Thailand.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    Even though no living Giant Sawfish are currently known in Thai waters, legal protections and preparedness measures remain in place:

    **Legal protection**
    - Protected wildlife under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2562.
    - Listed on **CITES Appendix I** — absolute prohibition on international commercial trade.
    - Import and export restrictions under the Import-Export Act B.E. 2522.

    **Fisheries management**
    Restrictions on bottom trawls and push nets in coastal waters reduce the likelihood of bycatch of large benthic species should any sawfish return to Thai waters in the future.

    **National action plan**
    The National Plan of Action for the Conservation and Management of Sharks in Thailand (NPOA-Sharks) includes the Giant Sawfish and provides a framework for long-term conservation planning.

    **Sighting network and data collection**
    A national network for reporting rare marine animal sightings has been established to ensure that any future encounter with a Giant Sawfish is immediately documented and reported to the DMCR, providing critical data for a potential recovery program.

    <Info>
      If you ever encounter what appears to be a Giant Sawfish saw rostrum for sale, it is a protected item and its sale is illegal in Thailand under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act. Report such sightings to the Department of Marine and Coastal Resources (DMCR).
    </Info>
  </Tab>
