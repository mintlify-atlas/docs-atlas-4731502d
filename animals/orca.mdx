<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Introduction">
    The Orca, or Killer Whale, is the **largest member of the oceanic dolphin family** and the **apex predator of the marine ecosystem**. Its body is powerful and stocky with a rounded head and a short beak. Its most recognizable features are its large paddle-like pectoral fins and its prominent dorsal fin — which in adult males can reach **2 meters in height** and stands upright, while in females the dorsal fin is shorter and curved.

    The Orca's jaws carry 10–14 pairs of large, rounded, slightly curved teeth on both upper and lower jaws.

    **Ecological role**
    The Orca is a natural predator of many of the other species featured in this project, including manta rays. When an Orca pursues prey, the target animal often panics and flees toward shallow water. This flight response is one of the recognized causes of **decompression sickness** (from ascending too rapidly) and **strandings** in prey species.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Threats">
    While the Orca is itself a predator rather than primary prey, it faces long-term pressures common to large cetaceans:

    **As a threat to other species**
    The Orca's pursuit of prey — dolphins, dugongs, sea turtles, and rays — is a documented natural factor leading to prey strandings in Thai waters. Panicked prey animals dive rapidly, suffer decompression sickness, and beach themselves.

    **Threats to the Orca itself**
    - **Marine pollution and plastic ingestion** — Like other cetaceans, Orcas are vulnerable to ingesting marine debris and accumulating toxins through the food chain.
    - **Noise pollution** — Sonar, vessel noise, and seismic surveys interfere with echolocation and communication, potentially leading to disorientation.
    - **Bycatch** — Accidental entanglement in commercial fishing gear, although less common than for slower species.
    - **Natural illness** — Disease is a documented cause of stranding.

    **Mass stranding risk**
    Orcas are highly social animals. When one animal in a pod is injured, ill, or navigates into shallow water, others frequently follow — leading to mass strandings.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Conservation">
    **Legal protection**
    All whales and dolphins in Thai waters, including the Orca, are protected under the Wildlife Conservation and Protection Act B.E. 2535 to prevent extinction.

    **Stranding response**
    When a live stranded whale is found, responders follow a standard protocol:
    1. Support the animal's body in the water to allow it to maintain normal orientation. Take extreme care not to allow water to enter the blowhole.
    2. If the animal is on land, dig support holes under each pectoral fin to reduce pressure on internal organs. Cover the body with wet cloth (leave the blowhole open) and douse it with water continuously to maintain moisture and reduce body temperature.

    **Mass stranding management**
    Mass strandings may be triggered by a lead animal navigating incorrectly. Managing them requires:
    - Rapid mobilization of personnel and a clear operational plan.
    - Herding and supporting the pod to guide them back to deep water simultaneously.
    - Ensuring that mother–calf pairs are kept together to prevent the calf from driving the mother back to shore.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>
